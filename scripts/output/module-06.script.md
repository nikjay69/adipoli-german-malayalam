## [13:30] The Magic Word: GERN
[VISUAL: Goethe Kochi practice corner. Kuttan uses printed photos of a German WG kitchen to demonstrate how to use "gern" with different actions while staying in Kerala.]

**EN:** Now, machane, I want to teach you one of the most useful words in all of German.
**EN:** Now, friend, I want to teach you one of the most useful words in all of German.

**EN:** Gern. It means "gladly" or "with pleasure." But in practice, it means "I LIKE doing something."

**EN:** Here is the formula: Subject + Verb + gern + object.

[VOCAB CARD: gern / gerne — gladly / to like (doing) — ഇഷ്ടത്തോടെ]
**DE example:** Ich esse gern Curry.
**EN gloss:** I like to eat curry.

**DE:** Ich TRINKE gern Kaffee. I LIKE TO DRINK coffee.
**EN:** Ich TRINKE gern Kaffee. I LIKE TO DRINK coffee.

**DE:** Ich ESSE gern Reis. I LIKE TO EAT rice.
**EN:** Ich ESSE gern Reis. I LIKE TO EAT rice.

**DE:** Stefan TRINKT gern Bier. Stefan LIKES TO DRINK beer.
**EN:** Stefan likes to drink beer.

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* "Gern" verb-kkondiye povuka. Position 2 — this is very important!

**EN:** Let me say that again: gern comes AFTER the verb. Never before it.

**DE:** Not: "Ich gern esse" — wrong!
**EN:** Not: "Ich gern esse" — wrong!

**DE:** But: "Ich esse gern" — correct!
**EN:** But: "Ich esse gern" — correct!

**EN:** The verb is always in position 2. Gern is its happy companion in position 3.

## [27:30] The Accusative Case — Der Akkusativ
[VISUAL: A graphic appears showing the accusative case changes. Then back to Kuttan in the restaurant, about to pay the bill.]

**EN:** Machane, now we enter the tricky part of German grammar. But I will make it simple, I promise!
**EN:** Friend, now we enter the tricky part of German grammar. But I will make it simple, I promise!

**EN:** In German, nouns change their articles depending on whether they are the SUBJECT or the OBJECT of a sentence.

**EN:** The SUBJECT is the one doing the action. The OBJECT is the one receiving the action.

**DE:** For example: "Ich trinke den Kaffee."
**EN:** For example: "Ich trinke den Kaffee."

**DE:** "Ich" — I — am the subject. I am drinking.
**EN:** "Ich" — I — am the subject. I am drinking.

**DE:** "Den Kaffee" — the coffee — is the object. The coffee is being drunk.
**EN:** "Den Kaffee" — the coffee — is the object. The coffee is being drunk.

**EN:** So when the noun is the object, we use the ACCUSATIVE case.

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Object-aayiulla noun article maarum. Ithaanu accusative case!

**EN:** Now here is the good news, machane: ONLY the MASCULINE articles change!
**EN:** Now here is the good news, friend: ONLY the MASCULINE articles change!

**DE:** Der becomes Den. Ein becomes Einen.
**EN:** Der becomes Den. Ein becomes Einen.

**EN:** Feminine, neuter, and plural — they ALL stay the same!

**EN:** This is a huge relief! Only masculine does the extra work. Think of it as masculine being the "drama queen" of German grammar!

[VOCAB CARD: den — the (masculine accusative) — ആ (പുല്ലിംഗം, കർമ്മം)]
**DE example:** Ich esse den Fisch.
**EN gloss:** I eat the fish.

[VOCAB CARD: einen — a (masculine accusative) — ഒരു (പുല്ലിംഗം, കർമ്മം)]
**DE example:** Ich nehme einen Kaffee.
**EN gloss:** I'll have a coffee.

**EN:** Let me say this once more, very clearly: DER becomes DEN. EIN becomes EINEN. Only masculine. Only when it is the object.
**EN:** Let me say this once more, very clearly: DER becomes DEN. EIN becomes EINEN. Only masculine. Only when it is the object.

**EN:** What are the masculine objects in our food vocabulary?

**DE:** Der Reis. Der Kaffee. Der Tee. Der Saft. Der Fisch. Der Wein. Der Käse.
**EN:** Der Reis. Der Kaffee. Der Tee. Der Saft. Der Fisch. Der Wein. Der Käse.

**EN:** When you EAT or DRINK these things, they become the object.

**DE example:** Ich trinke den Kaffee.
**EN gloss:** I drink the coffee.

**DE example:** Ich esse den Fisch.
**EN gloss:** I eat the fish.

**DE example:** Ich nehme einen Tee.
**EN gloss:** I will take a tea.
