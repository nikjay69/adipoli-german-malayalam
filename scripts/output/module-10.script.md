## [07:00] Grammar Point: tut weh vs tun weh
[VISUAL: Two comparative panels — "singular" and "plural" with examples]

Okay machane, now for the magic grammar. You know how to say "Mein Kopf tut weh" — my head hurts. But what if BOTH your eyes hurt?

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Nalla work! German-il plural varumbol verb change aavum. Let's see the pattern.

In German, when ONE body part hurts, you use "tut weh."

**DE:** Mein Kopf tut weh.
**EN:** My head hurts. (Singular)

When TWO or MORE body parts hurt, you use "tun weh."

**DE:** Meine Augen tun weh.
**EN:** My eyes hurt. (Plural)

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Pattern-simple aanu! One body part = "tut", two or more = "tun". Ithu workout-reps poley aayirikum practice-il!

Let me give you a few more examples so this clicks.

**DE:** Mein Rücken tut weh. (One back)
**EN:** My back hurts.

**DE:** Meine Beine tun weh. (Two legs)
**EN:** My legs hurt.

**DE:** Meine Ohren tun weh. (Two ears)
**EN:** My ears hurt.

**DE:** Mein Arm tut weh. (One arm)
**EN:** My arm hurts.

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Nalla work! Ithu German compound-word logic pole aanu. Sradhikkuka, practice cheyuka, machane!

## [15:30] Grammar Point: The Dativ Trap
[VISUAL: Two panels comparing CORRECT and WRONG expressions, with warning colors]

Now, machane, this is VERY important. Listen carefully.

In German, when we talk about PHYSICAL FEELINGS, we use DATIV, not Nominativ. That means we say "Mir ist..." not "Ich bin..."

Let me show you the traps!

**WRONG:** Ich bin kalt. (I am cold-hearted / I'm a cold person)
**CORRECT:** Mir ist kalt. (I feel cold)

**WRONG:** Ich bin schlecht. (I am a bad person!)
**CORRECT:** Mir ist schlecht. (I feel nauseous / unwell)

**WRONG:** Ich bin warm. (This can mean "I am gay" in slang!)
**CORRECT:** Mir ist warm. (I feel warm)

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Aiyyo! "Ich bin schlecht" paranjaal nee villain aakum! Never use that for health!*

The reason is: in German, physical sensations happen TO you. They are external events. So we use Dativ — "Mir" — to show the sensation is acting upon you.

This is the Dativ Trap. Don't fall into it!

**DE:** Mir ist kalt.
**EN:** I feel cold.

**DE:** Mir ist warm.
**EN:** I feel warm.

**DE:** Mir ist schlecht.
**EN:** I feel unwell.

**DE:** Mir ist übel.
**EN:** I feel nauseous.

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* Nalla work! Ithu valare important aanu — write it down, machane!

## [35:30] Grammar Point: gegen — Against
[VISUAL: Pharmacist behind counter, animated medicine boxes]

This is the KEY phrase for asking for medicine in German:

"Ich brauche etwas gegen..."

Something AGAINST...

**DE:** Ich brauche etwas gegen Kopfschmerzen.
**EN:** I need something against headaches.

**DE:** Ich brauche etwas gegen Husten.
**EN:** I need something for a cough.

**DE:** Ich brauche etwas gegen Schnupfen.
**EN:** I need something for a cold.

*Kuttan (മലയാളം):* "Gegen" means AGAINST! In German, medicine fights AGAINST the illness. "Für" (for) use cheyyaan pattiyatte!*

Why "gegen"? Because German thinks of medicine as fighting AGAINST the problem. It's a battle! So: "etwas gegen Kopfschmerzen" — something to fight your headache.
