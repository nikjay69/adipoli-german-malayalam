## Section 2: Situation 1 — The Office Mock (Am Arbeitsplatz) (1:00–3:00)

### Visual: [SCENE: Goethe Kochi classroom. Desks rearranged into a pretend office. Frau Weber sits as "Herr Schmidt, the manager". Kuttan approaches.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: cautious, professional]
"Scenario one. Frau Weber plays Herr Schmidt, a German manager. Kuttan walks in on his pretend first day.

**Kuttan:** Guten Morgen, Herr Schmidt. Haben **Sie** meine E-Mail erhalten? (Did you receive my email?)
**Frau Weber (as boss):** Ja, danke! Und wie geht es **Ihnen**, Herr Kumar?

Here's the rule for the office:
**Always start with 'Sie' and 'Herr' or 'Frau' + Last Name.**

*Herr Schmidt, Frau Müller — ippozhum Last Name use cheyyanam. Formal aanu safety.*

But wait. Kuttan's cousin chimes in from Munich on video: *'At my startup, everyone says du from day one, machaa. Tech companies are looser.'* Frau Weber nods: *'Correct — if the culture is casual, follow it. But with the boss, wait for them to offer du first.'*

### How to know?
Look at how others address each other.
If everyone is saying 'du', you can too.
But with the boss? **Wait for them to offer.**

[EXAM-TIP]
In the 'Schreiben' part of the exam, if you're writing to a company or a boss, you MUST use the formal **Sie** version of the letter."

## Section 3: Situation 2 — The University Mock (An der Uni) (3:00–5:00)

### Visual: [SCENE: Goethe Kochi classroom rearranged as a lecture hall. Frau Weber plays the professor this time, Akhil + Reshma play classmates. Cousin listens in from video.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: academic, structured]
"If you're a student, the university has a double-rule.

### With Professors:
Always, always **Sie**.
'Herr Professor Müller, können **Sie** mir bitte helfen?'

### With fellow students:
Almost always **du**.
'Hi! Bist **du** neu hier?'

Even if the student is 10 years older than you, **du** is the standard in German universities.

*Nammude college-ile polle thanne. Professor enu 'Ningal' parayum, classmate-inu 'Nee'.*

⏸️ **Try it:** Ask a classmate 'How are you?' ⏸️ 🔊 **'Wie geht's dir?'**
⏸️ **Try it:** Ask a professor 'How are you?' ⏸️ 🔊 **'Wie geht es Ihnen?'**"

## Section 4: Situation 3 — Shops, Cafés & Strangers (role-play over video) (5:00–7:00)

### Visual: [SCENE: Kuttan at his home desk, video call with cousin in Munich. Cousin plays a German shop clerk.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: brisk, practical]
"On the street, in a shop, or stopping a stranger for directions.

The rule: **Sie** is your bridge.

- **Staff:** Kann ich **Ihnen** helfen? (Can I help you?)
- **You:** Ja, danke. Verkaufen **Sie** auch SIM-Karten? (Do you also sell SIM cards?)

Wait — what about the bartender in a casual pub?
Often, bartenders use **du** with everyone. It's the 'beer culture'. 🍻

But as a newcomer, it is NEVER wrong to be too formal. It is often wrong to be too casual.

*Stranger-odu 'Entschuldigung, hast du Zeit?' ennu chodikkunnathinekal 'Haben Sie Zeit?' aanu mass!*

⏸️ **Practice:** Stop a stranger for the time. ⏸️ 🔊 **'Entschuldigung, haben Sie Zeit?'**"

## Section 5: The "Herr" and "Frau" Switch (7:00–8:00)

### Visual: [TABLE: First Name vs Last Name]
### Narration: [ENERGY: clear, focused]
"When you use **Sie**, you use the **Last Name**.
- **✅ Frau Müller**
- **❌ Frau Anna**

When you use **du**, you use the **First Name**.
- **✅ Anna**
- **❌ Müller**

In Kerala, we sometimes say 'Mr. Rahul' or 'Mrs. Anjali' using the first name. 
**Do NOT do this in Germany.** It sounds like a child talking to an adult.

Use the Last Name with Herr/Frau, or just the First Name without the title.

*First name use cheyyanal title venda. Last name use cheyyanal title venam. Athra ulu.*"

## Section 6: Section Summary — Your Respect Compass (8:00–9:15)

### Visual: [Compass graphic pointing to different zones]
### Narration: [ENERGY: summary, direct]
"Here is your compass:

- **Formal Zone (Sie):** Immigration, Doctors, Professors, Bosses, Strangers over 20.
- **Casual Zone (du):** Family, Close Friends, Kids, Classmates, Pub staff (sometimes).

[EXAM-TIP]
In the 'Sprechen' test, when you introduce yourself, the examiner might say:
'Können **Sie** Ihren Namen buchstabieren?' (Can you spell your name?)
The examiner uses **Sie**. You must always answer with the same formal respect.

🔊 **Memorize the formal 'Help me' line:**
'Können Sie mir bitte helfen?'
