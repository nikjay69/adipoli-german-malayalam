## Section 2: Question 1 — Age (0:50–2:15)

### Visual: [SLIDE: "Wie alt bist du?" (Informal / Wie alt sind Sie? (Formal). Formula for age.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: clear, instructional]
"The first question is always age.

🔊 **'Wie alt bist du?'** (How old are you? — casual)
🔊 **'Wie alt sind Sie?'** (How old are you? — formal)

The answer is simple: **'Ich bin [Number] Jahre alt.'**

*Note: In the exam, numbers matter! We practiced them in Module 1. If you're 24, say 'vierundzwanzig'.*

⏸️ **Try: Say your age in German! (Ich bin ... Jahre alt.)**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (3 seconds)
**Ich bin ... Jahre alt.** ✅"

---

## Section 3: Question 2 — Residence & Living (2:15–3:30)

### Visual: [SCENE: Map highlighting your current German city. Question text appearing.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: informative, practical]
"Where do you actually stay?

🔊 **'Wo wohnst du?'** (Where do you live? — casual)
🔊 **'Wo wohnen Sie?'** (Where do you live? — formal)

Answer: **'Ich wohne in [Stadt].'** (Ich wohne in Berlin. Ich wohne in Kochi.)

*Wait — is it 'wohnen' or 'leben'? 'Wohnen' is for your apartment or house. 'Leben' is for the general city or life. Usually, 'wohnen' is the safer answer in A1.*

⏸️ **Say it: I live in Munich!**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**Ich wohne in München.** ✅"

## Section 4: Question 3 — Marital Status (3:30–5:30)

### Visual: [TABLE: verheiratet (married), ledig (single), geschieden (divorced). Wedding-ring and lone-person icons.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: gentle, conversational]
"Germans are direct. They will ask if you're married. Don't be offended!

🔊 **'Sind Sie verheiratet?'** (Are you married? — formal)
🔊 **'Bist du verheiratet?'** (Are you married? — casual)

[Three answers appear]
1. **'Ja, ich bin verheiratet.'** (Yes, I am married.)
2. **'Nein, ich bin ledig.'** (No, I am single.) — *'Ledig' like in 'landing'!*
3. **'Ich bin geschieden.'** (I am divorced.)

*Ithu nammude personal info forms-ilum undakum. Forms-il 'Familiestand' ennu kandaal, ivithe oru word ezhuthuka.*

⏸️ **Say 'I am single' in German!**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**Ich bin ledig.** ✅"

## Section 5: Question 4 — Siblings (5:30–7:00)

### Visual: [SCENE: Group of brothers and sisters. Question text appearing.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: warm, family-vibes]
"Do you have brothers or sisters?

🔊 **'Hast du Geschwister?'** (Do you have siblings? — casual)
🔊 **'Haben Sie Geschwister?'** (Haben Sie Geschwister? — formal)

[Answer blocks]
- **'Ja, ich habe einen Bruder.'** (Yes, I have a brother.)
- **'Ja, ich habe eine Schwester.'** (Yes, I have a sister.)
- **'Nein, ich habe keine Geschwister.'** (No, I have no siblings.)

*In Kerala, we almost always have a sibling or a cousin we call a 'brother'. Use **'Bruder' / 'Schwester'** for the real ones!*

⏸️ **Do you have siblings? Say it in German!**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (3 seconds)
**Ja, ich habe... / Nein, ich habe keine...** ✅"

## Section 6: Question 5 — Time in Germany (7:00–8:30)

### Visual: [SCENE: Calendar showing arrival date. 1 month, 6 months, 1 year icons.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: empathetic, welcoming]
"People will ask: how long have you been here?

🔊 **'Wie lange bist du schon in Deutschland?'** (How long have you been in Germany?)

Answer with **'Seit'** (Since/For):
- **'Seit einem Monat.'** (For one month.)
- **'Seit sechs Monaten.'** (For six months.)
- **'Ich bin neu hier.'** (I am new here.)

*Always say 'Ich bin neu hier' if you just arrived. People will be EXTRA helpful.*

⏸️ **Say it: I've been here for 3 months! (Seit drei Monaten.)**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**Seit drei Monaten.** ✅"

## Section 7: Malayalam Logic — Handling Directness (8:30–9:30)

### Visual: [TABLE: Malayali indirectness vs German directness.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: reassuring, cultural]
"In Kerala, we might ask 'Are you staying alone?' or 'Which house?' before 'Are you married?'. Germans go straight to the marital status.

Don't feel like you're being interrogated! For them, it's just 'getting to know you' data. Keep your answers short and polite. You don't need to give a speech — just a simple 'Ja' or 'Nein' with the fact is perfect.

[EXAM-TIP] If an examiner asks a follow-up you don't understand, don't just say 'No'. Say: **'Wie bitte? Noch einmal bitte?'** (Pardon? Once more please?) It's better to ask for a repeat than to guess!"
