## Section 2: The Date Format — Spot the Dots (0:50–1:45)

### Visual: [DIAGRAM: Tag . Monat . Jahr (DD.MM.YYYY). Icons for Kerala and Germany indicating match.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: clear, simple]
"Good news! The German date format is exactly the same as in India.

[Formula appears]
**DD.MM.YYYY**

- **05.10.1995** — Fifth of October, 1995.

*Americans reverse it, but Germans and Indians are on the same page. Nammal pen-eduthu ezhuthuna pole thanne!*

[Note]: We use **dots (.)**, not slashes (/). In German, that dot is the sign for an 'Ordinal Number' (First, Second, Third)."

## Section 3: Ordinal Numbers — The '-te' Rule (1:45–4:00)

### Visual: [TABLE: Number | Ordinal | Pronunciation. Red highlights for irregulars.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: focused, instructional]
"To say 'First', 'Second', 'Third', we add a small ending: **-te**.

[Irregulars first]
🔊 **1.** = **erste** (First) - *Not 'einste'!*
🔊 **3.** = **dritte** (Third) - *Not 'dreite'!*
🔊 **7.** = **siebte** (Seventh).
🔊 **8.** = **achte** (Eighth).

[The regulars — 2 to 19]
Just add **-te**.
- **heute ist der zweite** (2.)
- **heute ist der vierte** (4.)

[The 20+ jump]
From 20 upwards, we add **-ste**.
- **der zwanzigste** (20.)
- **der dreißigste** (30.)

⏸️ **Try: 5th?**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**fünfte.** ✅

⏸️ **1st?**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**erste.** ✅"

## Section 4: The Verb 'haben' — Our Second Power Verb (4:00–6:30)

### Visual: [TABLE: full conjugation of 'haben'. Highlight on the 'h' disappearing in du/er.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: building, teaching]
"We meet our second most used verb: **haben** (to have).

You need it for:
- 'Ich habe Geburtstag.' (I have a birthday.)
- 'Ich habe einen Termin.' (I have an appointment.)
- 'Ich habe Zeit.' (I have time.)

[Conjugation table appears]
- **ich habe**
- **du hast** 💡 *(The 'b' disappears!)*
- **er / sie / es hat** 💡 *(The 'b' disappears!)*
- **wir haben**
- **ihr habt**
- **sie / Sie haben**

*Wait — du hast, er hat. Ithu eppolum 'b' illathe parayanam. 'Habe' ennu paranjaal athu mistake aanu.*

⏸️ **Try: I have?**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**ich habe.** ✅

⏸️ **He has?**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**er hat.** ✅"

## Section 5: "Wann hast du Geburtstag?" (6:30–8:30)

### Visual: [SCENE: Two people chatting. Speech bubbles: "Wann hast du Geburtstag?" Answer formula appearing.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: practical, conversational]
"In the exam, they might ask: **'Wann haben Sie Geburtstag?'**

Answer: **'Mein Geburtstag ist am [Number]. [Month].'**

[Examples]
🔊 **'Mein Geburtstag ist am fünften Januar.'**
🔊 **'Mein Geburtstag ist am ersten August.'**

*Note: In the full sentence 'am fünften', we add an 'n' to the end. Don't worry about why right now (it's the Dativ case!), just memorize the sound: **'am ...-ten'**.*

⏸️ **Your Turn: Say when YOUR birthday is! (Mein Geburtstag ist am ... [Month].)**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (5 seconds)
**Mein Geburtstag ist am ...** ✅"

## Section 6: Malayalam Logic — "Has" vs "Is" (8:30–9:30)

### Visual: [TABLE: Malayalam 'Enikku ... undu' vs German 'Ich habe'.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: cultural, relatable]
"In Malayalam, we say **'Enikku birthday ippol undu'**.
In German, we say **'Ich habe Geburtstag'**.

They both use the sense of 'owning' or 'having'. It's very similar!

[EXAM-TIP] In the **Schreiben** (writing) part, they ask for your 'Geburtsdatum' (date of birth) on forms. Write it like this: **12.04.1990**. Use dots! It looks professional and correct."
