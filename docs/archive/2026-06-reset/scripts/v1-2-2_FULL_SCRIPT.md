## Section 3: The 7 Trap Letters (1:30–3:30)

### 🎬 [Energy: focused, one-at-a-time reveals]
### Visual: [Each letter appears alone with its German name, then English comparison]
### Narration:
"These 7 letters will trip you up because English trained your brain wrong.

### Letter 1: **J** = 'yot'
NOT 'jay'. **Yot.**
🔊 *yot*

### Letter 2: **W** = 'vay'
NOT 'double-u'. **Vay.**
🔊 *vay*

### Letter 3: **V** = 'fow'
NOT 'vee'. **Fow.**
🔊 *fow*

Think about that: W and V swap compared to English! W = vay, V = fow. *Confusing aanu, but ippo thanne fix cheyyaam.*

### Letter 4: **Z** = 'tset'
NOT 'zee'. **Tset.**
🔊 *tset*

### Letter 5: **Y** = 'üpsilon'
NOT 'why'. **Üpsilon.**
🔊 *üpsilon*

### Letter 6: **R** = 'err'
Rolled or uvular, NOT the flat English R.
🔊 *err*

### Letter 7: **S** = 'ess'
Same as English — but remember, German **ß** is called 'Eszett' or 'scharfes S'.
🔊 *Eszett*

---

### ⏸️ Active Recall — Trap Letters
I say the English name, you say the German name:

1. 'Jay' → ⏸️ → **Yot!**
2. 'Double-u' → ⏸️ → **Vay!**
3. 'Vee' → ⏸️ → **Fow!**
4. 'Zee' → ⏸️ → **Tset!**

*Sherikkum important aanu ee four — Amt-il ithokke verum confuse cheyyum if you use English names.*"

## Section 5: Spell Your Name — The Core Skill (4:45–6:15)

### 🎬 [Energy: practical, coaching tone]
### Visual: [Name cards appearing letter by letter as spelled]
### Narration:
"Now the skill that matters most: spelling your name.

Let me show you with common Malayali names.

### Example 1: **NIKHIL**
🔊 *N — I — K — H — I — L*
*En — ee — ka — ha — ee — ell*

Simple — no trap letters. But practice saying it smoothly.

### Example 2: **JAYASREE**
🔊 *J — A — Y — A — S — R — E — E*
*Yot — ah — üpsilon — ah — ess — err — ay — ay*

See? J and Y are the traps. If you say 'jay' instead of 'yot', the clerk writes something else.

### Example 3: **VISHNU**
🔊 *V — I — S — H — N — U*
*Fow — ee — ess — ha — en — oo*

V = fow! Not 'vee'.

## Section 6: Buchstabiertafel — The Spelling Alphabet (6:15–7:30)

### 🎬 [Energy: informative, culture-note tone]
### Visual: [Table showing A wie Anton, B wie Berta, etc. — key letters only]
### Narration:
"On the phone, letters can sound similar. B and P? D and T? M and N?

Germans solve this with the **Buchstabiertafel** — a spelling alphabet.

Like NATO's 'Alpha Bravo Charlie', but German style:

🔊 **A wie Anton**
🔊 **B wie Berta**
🔊 **D wie Dora**
🔊 **M wie Martha**
🔊 **N wie Nordpol**
🔊 **S wie Samuel**

You say: '*N wie Nordpol, I wie Ida, K wie Kaufmann, H wie Heinrich, I wie Ida, L wie Ludwig*' — and the clerk gets NIKHIL perfectly.

### [EXAM-TIP] 📋
You won't need the full Buchstabiertafel for A1. But knowing 'wie' (as in) helps:
- *'V wie Viktor'* — instantly clear, no confusion with W.
- *'J wie Julius'* — so no one writes G.

For now, just remember the pattern: **letter + wie + name**.

*Phone call-il name parayumbol ee trick use cheyyaam. 'Mein Name ist Nikhil. N wie Nordpol, I wie Ida...' — professional aayittum clear aayittum sound cheyyum.*"

## Section 7: Phone Numbers in German (7:30–8:45)

### 🎬 [Energy: practical, step-by-step]
### Visual: [Phone number appearing digit by digit, then in pairs]
### Narration:
"One more survival skill: saying phone numbers.

In Germany, phone numbers are usually read in **pairs**, not single digits.

### Example: 0176 — 34 — 56 — 78 — 90

🔊 *Null eins sieben sechs — vierunddreißig — sechsundfünfzig — achtundsiebzig — neunzig*

### The pattern:
- Area code / mobile prefix: digit by digit
- The rest: in pairs

### Single digits for the prefix:
🔊 *null — eins — sieben — sechs*

### Pairs use the numbers from Module 3:
🔊 *vierunddreißig* (34) — *sechsundfünfzig* (56)

*Numbers Module 3-il padichathum ippol use aavunnu!*

---

### ⏸️ Practice — Say This Number
📱 **0151 — 23 — 45 — 67 — 89**

Try it now. Prefix digit by digit, then pairs.

⏸️ [5-second pause]

🔊 *Null eins fünf eins — dreiundzwanzig — fünfundvierzig — siebenundsechzig — neunundachtzig*

How did you do? If the pairs feel hard, go back to Module 3 for a refresher — no shame in that!"
