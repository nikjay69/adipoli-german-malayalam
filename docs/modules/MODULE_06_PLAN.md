# Module 06: Food & Drink (Essen und Trinken)

## Teaching Goal
After this module, students should be able to **name common foods and drinks with articles**, **order food politely at a restaurant**, **express likes and dislikes**, and **use the accusative case in practical food/ordering contexts**. This module teaches grammar through enjoyable, high-frequency real-life interactions.

## A1 Exam Relevance

| Exam Section | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Hören** | Café/restaurant/shop conversations are common in listening tasks. Order exchanges and preference questions appear regularly. |
| **Lesen** | Menus, signs, simple food-related notices, supermarket ads. |
| **Sprechen** | Ordering, requesting, saying likes/dislikes — practical interaction tasks. |
| **Grammar** | Accusative case is highly teachable through food choices: "Ich nehme EINEN Kaffee" — the article change becomes concrete. |

## Essential Outcomes
Students must be able to **produce** (not just recognize):
1. Name at least 20 common foods and drinks with correct articles
2. Order food and drinks politely: "Ich hätte gern einen Kaffee und ein Brötchen, bitte."
3. Express likes: "Ich esse gern Reis" / dislikes: "Ich mag keinen Fisch"
4. Use accusative articles correctly in ordering sentences (einen/eine/ein)
5. Handle a basic restaurant dialogue: greeting → ordering → paying → goodbye
6. Read and understand a simple German menu

## Gap Analysis
- **No speaking exercises** (0/32) — ordering food is inherently a speaking task
- **Only 1 ordering exercise** — restaurant dialogue needs much more production practice
- **No menu-reading comprehension** — should simulate reading a real German menu
- **No dictation** — listening to an order and noting items
- **Accusative practice is limited** — needs more varied production, not just recognition of article changes
- **No role-play** customer/server exercises
- **Missing Kerala food vocabulary** — Malayali students will want to describe their own cuisine (Biryani, Dosa, Sambar, Chai)
- **No connection to Module 7** (shopping/prices) — food shopping bridges naturally
- **Tipping culture** taught but not practiced in exercises

## Future Script Direction

### Suggested Narrative Approach
- Use food as a fun confidence-builder while quietly teaching accusative grammar
- "Germans take their bread VERY seriously — more than 3,000 types of bread!"
- Build restaurant dialogue as a role-play that students can actually use on their first day in Germany
- Accusative should feel like a natural part of ordering, not an abstract grammar concept

### Malayalam Bridge Points
- **Tea/Chai culture** — "Germans drink Kaffee like Malayalis drink Chai — it's a ritual!"
- **Rice as staple** — "Reis is as important in South India as Brot is in Germany"
- **Spice awareness** — "German food might feel bland at first — 'nicht scharf' is the default!"
- **Kerala cuisine vocabulary** — help students describe their food culture: "In Kerala essen wir viel Reis und Curry"
- **Hospitality** — Kerala's "Virunnu" (feast) culture vs German Einladung etiquette
- **Vegetarian options** — "Germany is getting very vegetarian-friendly — 'Ich bin Vegetarier' is a powerful phrase!"

### Key Example Sentences/Dialogues to Feature
```
Ordering:
Kellner: Guten Tag! Was möchten Sie?
Gast: Ich hätte gern eine Suppe und einen Salat, bitte.
Kellner: Und zu trinken?
Gast: Ein Wasser, bitte.
Kellner: Kommt sofort!
...
Gast: Die Rechnung, bitte.
Kellner: Das macht zwölf Euro fünfzig.
Gast: Bitte schön. Stimmt so! (Keep the change)
Kellner: Vielen Dank! Auf Wiedersehen!

Preferences:
"Ich esse gern Reis und Curry.
Ich trinke gern Tee, aber keinen Kaffee.
Mein Lieblingsessen ist Biryani.
Ich mag keinen Fisch."

Accusative Practice:
"Ich nehme einen Kaffee." (der Kaffee → einen)
"Ich esse eine Suppe." (die Suppe → eine)
"Ich trinke ein Bier." (das Bier → ein)
"Ich möchte den Kuchen." (der Kuchen → den)
```
