## Section 2: Getting the Attention — "Zahlen, bitte!" (0:50–2:30)

### Visual: [HIGHLIGHT: 1. "Die Rechnung, bitte!" | 2. "Zahlen, bitte!". Icon: A person waving politely.]

- 🔊 **'Zahlen, bitte!'** — *Literally: 'Paying, please!'. Very common and efficient.*

## Section 3: The Big Question — Together or Separate? (2:30–4:30)

### Visual: [DIAGRAM: 1. Zusammen (One person pays) | 2. Getrennt (Each person pays their own). Soundwave for the pronunciation.]
### Narration: [ENERGY: building, logical]
"When the waiter arrives, they will ask:
🔊 **'Zusammen oder getrennt?'** (Together or separate?)

[Options]
1. 🔊 **'Zusammen, bitte.'** — *We pay together. (One person pays).*
2. 🔊 **'Getrennt, bitte.'** — *We pay separately.*

*German culture-il 'getrennt' pay cheyyunnathu valare common aanu. Aarum athu mosham-aayi karuthilla!*

## Section 4: The Tipping Art — "Stimmt so!" (4:30–6:30)

### Visual: [ILLUSTRATION: Bill is 18.50. Customer says "Zwanzig" (20). Formula: [Actual Price] + [Tip] = [What you say].]
### Narration: [ENERGY: attentive, cultural]
"Tipping in Germany is not 20% like in the US. It's usually a **rounding up** move.

[The "Stimmt so" Rule]
If the bill is **18.50 Euro**:
- You give a 20 Euro note and say: 🔊 **'Stimmt so!'** (Keep the change!).
- This means you are giving a 1.50 Euro tip.

[The "State the Total" Rule]
If you want to give exactly 1 Euro tip:
- You say: 🔊 **'Neunzehn fünfzig, bitte.'** (19.50, please).
- They will give you 50 cents back from your 20.

*In India, nammal 'Keep the change' ennu parayum pole. German-il 'Stimmt so!' ennu parayaam.*

⏸️ **Say: Keep the change!**
### Practice Pause: ⏸️ (2 seconds)
**Stimmt so!** ✅"

## Section 5: Malayalam Comparison — The Hospitality Difference (6:30–8:00)

### Visual: [TABLE: Kerala (Waiters are fast, tipping is small/fixed) vs. Germany (Waiters leave you alone, tipping is rounding up).]
### Narration: [ENERGY: cultural, structural]
"In Kerala, the bill often comes quickly so the next person can sit. In Germany, the table is yours all night!

Also, 'Zusammen oder getrennt' is a major difference. In a Malayali group, saying 'getrennt' might feel awkward. In Germany, it's the default for colleagues and friends.

*Language padikkumpol culture-um koode orkkuna-thaanu exam-il help cheyyukka!*

[EXAM-TIP] The examiner might ask: 'Wie möchten Sie zahlen?' (How would you like to pay?). Answer: 'Mit Karte, bitte' (With card) or 'Bar, bitte' (Cash)."
