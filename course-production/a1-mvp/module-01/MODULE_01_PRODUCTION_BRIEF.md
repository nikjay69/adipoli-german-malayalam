# Module 1 Production Brief — First German Moment

Updated: 2026-06-02 11:05 CEST

## Module promise

By the end of Module 1, a zero-level Malayali learner can **hear, understand, and answer a first polite German greeting**, pronounce the most dangerous beginner sounds, and survive a tiny formal exchange without freezing.

This module is not a motivation lecture. It is the first proof that the course works: within the first minute the learner hears German and says German.

## Customer outcome

A learner finishes Module 1 able to:

- recognise `Guten Morgen`, `Guten Tag`, `Guten Abend`, `Hallo`, `Auf Wiedersehen`;
- reply politely to a teacher/examiner/service person;
- say `Ich lerne Deutsch.` and `Noch einmal, bitte.`;
- understand the difference between formal `Sie` energy and casual `du` energy;
- pronounce high-risk sounds well enough for A1: `ch`, `sch`, German `w`, umlauts;
- complete a closed mini-check without notes;
- know exactly which recovery task to do if listening/speaking/pronunciation is weak.

## Lesson/video map

| # | Owned video lesson | Target length | Learner win | Main output |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| 1 | Start listening: `Guten Morgen` | 25m | Hear + answer first German line | Say `Guten Morgen, Frau Fischer.` |
| 2 | Why A1 matters for Malayalis | 25m | Understand the exam/course path | Say why they are learning in one sentence |
| 3 | German sounds for Malayalam speakers | 45m | Pronounce `ch/sch/w/v/ä/ö/ü` | Shadow 10 sound lines |
| 4 | Formal greetings | 35m | Choose greeting by time/context | Answer 8 greeting prompts |
| 5 | Politeness survival | 35m | Use `bitte/danke/Entschuldigung` | Repair rude/too-casual replies |
| 6 | Goodbye and repair phrases | 30m | Ask for repetition and exit politely | Say `Noch einmal, bitte.` |
| 7 | First mini-conversation | 40m | Complete tiny exchange | Greet → answer → thank → leave |

Total Module 1 owned video target: **240 minutes**.

## Must-do path

1. First scene: Frau Fischer says `Guten Morgen`; learner answers aloud.
2. Video 1: Boss explains the first greeting and why formal tone matters.
3. Practice 1: learner shadows 5 greeting lines.
4. Repair 1: learner fixes the wrong response: `Gute Nacht` vs `Guten Morgen`.
5. Video 2: short motivation/pathway lesson, not a long Germany pitch.
6. Video 3: sound training for Malayalam speakers.
7. Practice 2: `ich/sch`, `w/v`, umlaut recognition.
8. Videos 4–6: greetings, politeness, goodbye/repair phrases.
9. Video 7: first mini-conversation.
10. Module checkpoint: closed-book listening + speaking + tiny writing.
11. Recovery: app assigns 1–3 exact tasks if weak.

## Score-booster path

Only after pass/weak-pass or if the learner asks for more:

- One curated public pronunciation video for `ch/sch/ü` — output: record 8 words.
- One slow audio greeting drill — output: identify 20 greetings/responses.
- One Goethe speaking micro-card — output: examiner greets, learner answers formally.

No resource dump. Each booster has one resource, one output, one time box.

## Checkpoint and recovery tags

- `hoeren:greetings` — misses greeting/time/context.
- `sprechen:greeting_reply` — cannot answer aloud without freezing.
- `sprechen:formality` — uses casual tone where formal is safer.
- `pronunciation:ch_sch` — confuses `ich` / `isch`.
- `pronunciation:w_v` — pronounces German `w` like English w.
- `schreiben:first_sentence` — cannot write `Ich lerne Deutsch.` cleanly.
- `vocab:greeting_set` — weak recall of greeting/politeness words.

Recovery cards are short: reteach, drill, retest. No generic “revise Module 1”.

## Phone/desktop UX notes

Phone first:

- One visible action: `Play` or `Say it aloud`.
- No long paragraph before audio.
- Use subtitles only as support: `Frau Fischer: Guten Morgen.`
- Speaking self-check is acceptable for MVP; recording/manual scoring can come later.
- Hide global nav/search during focused lessons.

Desktop:

- Allow video + notes side by side.
- Keep the same linear path; do not turn desktop into a browsing dashboard.

## Production style

Boss will later record the explanation in Malayalam/Manglish. Therefore scripts should use:

- English stage directions;
- German examples exactly as displayed;
- `[Boss explains in Malayalam/Manglish]` placeholders for natural teacher talk;
- on-screen text that is short and mostly English/German;
- tables only when they reduce explanation;
- adult-safe Kerala learner visuals, not childish mascot scenes.
